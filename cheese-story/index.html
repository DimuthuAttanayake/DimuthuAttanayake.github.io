America's cheese divide: Walmart vs Whole Foods

Low prices sometimes come with a different cost.

By Dimuthu Attanayake

Cheese is an American household staple. According to the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA), on any given day, 68 percent of U.S. adults consume cheese. Cheese is woven into nearly everything Americans cook. Seventy percent of all cheese consumed arrives not as a standalone snack but inside sandwiches, pizza, tacos, burritos, and quesadillas. And then there is mac and cheese, the dish 54 percent of Americans eat at least once a week. In total, Americans eat a record 40.54 pounds of cheese per person annually, up five pounds in a decade.

Where consumers shop for these cheeses, however, says as much about their income as their palate. The core Walmart shopper earns under $80,000 a year, and 90 percent of the U.S. population lives within 10 miles of one. By contrast, nearly half of Whole Foods customers earn over $100,000 a year, with 93 percent of their stores concentrated in urban areas. This means that the cheese price divide is also wide. If they shop at Whole Foods instead of Walmart, they would pay at least $2.50 more for most varieties, with the markup exceeding $13 for specialty types like Gouda or Blue. Different cheeses serve different roles. Cheddar and American, the two most popular types, dominate mac and cheese, burgers, and grilled sandwiches, prized for their melt and tang. Mozzarella is a key ingredient in America’s pizza, cream cheese compliments the morning bagel, and parmesan the pasta. Feta and goat cheese appear in salads and grain bowls favoured by younger, specialty-seeking consumers.
